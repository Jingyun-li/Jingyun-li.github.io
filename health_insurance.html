Health Insurance Costs

Impact of Demographics and Lifestyle on Premium Pricing

full project accessible below

This study analyzes how demographic and behavioral factors — such as age, gender, BMI, number of children, smoking status, and region — affect health-insurance charges for U.S. policyholders. Using statistical modeling and regression diagnostics, we examined whether these predictors can accurately forecast an individual’s expected premium cost.

Data Description

The dataset ($n = 1{,}338$, $k = 7$) was sourced from an anonymous U.S. insurer and published on Kaggle. It includes numerical variables (age, BMI, charges) and categorical variables (sex, smoker, region). These attributes capture the main policyholder characteristics relevant to premium pricing.

  • Age, BMI, and number of children are continuous predictors.
  • Smoker status and region are categorical factors encoded as indicator variables.
  • No missing values were detected; minimal cleaning was required.

Methodology

Multiple linear regression models were fitted in R to estimate the relationship between insurance charges and demographic variables. Stepwise selection, partial F-tests, and Box–Cox transformations were used to refine model accuracy and verify the assumptions of linearity, homoscedasticity, and normality.

  • The initial full model achieved $R^2 \approx 0.75$ but showed non-normal residuals.
  • Log-transformations and interaction terms ($\text{BMI} \times \text{smoker}$) were tested to improve fit.
  • The final interaction model reached $R^2 = 0.84$ and adjusted $R^2 = 0.836$ ($p < 2\times10^{-16}$).

Findings

Smoking status was the dominant factor, increasing charges by over 200%. Age and BMI also showed strong positive effects. Introducing the $\text{BMI} \times \text{smoker}$ interaction captured the nonlinear effect of BMI among smokers, substantially improving predictive accuracy.

Key Insights

The refined model demonstrates that demographic and lifestyle variables can predict insurance charges with high reliability. Although residual normality remained imperfect due to right-skewed premium data, the model provides valuable insights for pricing strategies and preventive-health policy design.
