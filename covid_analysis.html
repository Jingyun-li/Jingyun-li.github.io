COVID-19 Data Analysis

Examining infection trends, mortality rates, and housing market correlations.

View Full Project

This project explores how the COVID-19 pandemic evolved across the United States using data from Johns Hopkins University. The analysis investigates infection and mortality trends, applies hypothesis testing on changing fatality rates, and examines how regional housing prices responded during the pandemic period.

Methodology

COVID-19 case and death data were aggregated at both national and state levels. Using Python and libraries such as Pandas, Matplotlib, and Scikit-learn, the project visualized cumulative growth, daily fluctuations, and cross-regional differences. A separate dataset from the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) provided regional housing price indices, which were combined with infection data for regression modeling.

  • Age, BMI, and number of children are continuous predictors.
  • Smoker status and region are categorical factors encoded as indicator variables.
  • No missing values were detected; minimal cleaning was required.

Methodology

Multiple linear regression models were fitted in R to estimate the relationship between insurance charges and demographic variables. Stepwise selection, partial F-tests, and Box–Cox transformations were used to refine model accuracy and verify assumptions of linearity, homoscedasticity, and normality.

Findings

  • Daily confirmed cases and deaths showed clear waves, peaking in mid-2020 and again in 2022, before stabilizing.
  • Hypothesis testing confirmed a statistically significant decline in mortality rate after mid-2021 ($p < 0.001$), consistent with vaccine rollout and improved treatments.
  • Regression results showed a strong positive correlation between COVID-19 case counts and regional housing price indices ($R^2 \approx 0.95$ nationwide).

Key Insights

The pandemic’s progression demonstrates both health and economic interdependence: infection rates shaped public behavior and housing demand, while falling mortality and recovery rates reflected medical advances. Overall, the data indicates resilience in housing markets despite the unprecedented disruption caused by COVID-19.
